Research Focus

Securities regulation, market microstructure, and enforcement in an era of algorithmic trading, prediction markets, and cross-border capital flows.

Technology & Algorithmic Governance

AI-driven decision-making, data privacy, platform regulation — balancing innovation with accountability and fairness.

Venture Capital & Startups

Private ordering, financing structures, and the legal architecture of startup ownership — from seed stage to IPO and beyond.

Consumer & Household Finance

Financial products, algorithmic pricing, and disclosure regimes — with attention to discrimination, behavioral biases, and the effects of fintech.

About the Initiative

The Columbia–Haifa Research Initiative is a collaborative research partnership between the University of Haifa Faculty of Law and Columbia Law School. Founded by Prof. Moran Ofir and Prof. Joshua Mitts, the Initiative brings together leading scholars, emerging researchers, and practitioners to study the legal architecture of modern markets and the governance of the technologies transforming them.

Our research agenda encompasses four interconnected domains: financial markets and regulation; technology and algorithmic governance; venture capital and startups; and consumer and household finance. These domains reflect the conviction that the most pressing questions in market governance today sit at the intersection of law, technology, and economics.

Why Columbia and Haifa

The partnership builds on deep institutional ties. Multiple members hold degrees from or have held positions at Columbia Law School, and the two faculties share a commitment to rigorous, empirically grounded, and policy-relevant legal scholarship. Israel's position as a global technology hub and its distinctive regulatory landscape make Haifa a natural partner for research on how law and markets interact in a technology-driven economy.

Featured Research

Market Efficiency and Processing Costs
Joshua Mitts & Moran Ofir
Columbia Law & Economics Working Paper (2025) · Featured on Harvard Law Forum

Examines how processing costs affect market efficiency by exploiting mandatory 30-minute trading halts on the Tel Aviv Stock Exchange for U.S.-Israel dual-listed stocks. Using intraday data from 2,117 halts across 96 securities, the study finds that U.S. markets experience significantly increased illiquidity and volatility during halts.

Financial Markets SSRN →
From Iran to Taylor Swift: Informed Trading in Prediction Markets
Joshua Mitts & Moran Ofir
First Draft (March 2026)

A systematic empirical and legal study of informed trading on Polymarket and Kalshi. Documents case studies spanning the U.S.-Israel strike on Iran to Taylor Swift's engagement. Develops a composite screening score analyzing over 210,000 suspicious wallet-market pairs, estimating $143 million in aggregate anomalous profit.

Financial Markets Technology
The Input Fallacy
Talia Gillis
Minnesota Law Review 106(3) (2022) · 2022 AALS Scholarly Papers Winner

Establishes that fair lending law's focus on scrutinizing algorithmic inputs fails to address discrimination in credit-pricing algorithms, creating an "algorithmic myth of colorblindness."

First empirical study of pay-to-play clauses in venture capital financing, examining how these provisions shape investor behavior and startup outcomes across jurisdictions.

Join the Initiative

We invite researchers and practitioners whose work intersects with our research pillars to join the Columbia–Haifa community.

Core Fellows

PhD students, postdocs, and junior faculty with active research agendas. Fellows present at workshops, attend seminars, and produce collaborative scholarship.

Academic Affiliates

Scholars from other institutions who complement the Initiative's research agenda. Participate in selected events and collaborate on specific projects.

Practitioner Affiliates

Professionals from financial regulation, legal practice, fintech, and the investment industry bringing real-world perspective to research.
